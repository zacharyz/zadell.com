> Outline. This is partly a meta-piece for a site that just got rebuilt on these principles — leaning into that is fine.
>
> **Voice notes:** Slightly polemical but quiet. The argument is strongest when the tone is restrained. Avoid "platforms bad, blogs good" — the more interesting claim is what *changed* recently.

**Suggested openings:**

- "For most of the last decade, the answer to *where do I publish this* was: somewhere else."
- A specific platform memory — the day Twitter changed its algorithm, the Medium paywall, the Substack acquisition rumor.
- "There has always been a quieter strand of the internet. It never went away — it just got quieter."

---

## The platform decade

[Set up the historical frame. Briefly. Don't dwell on the obvious.]

- Twitter, Medium, LinkedIn, Substack — the answer to "where do I publish" was always *somewhere else.*
- The personal site became a marquee pointing at the writing happening elsewhere.
- This was the deal. We took it.

## What broke

[The pivot. What changed that made the deal feel worse.]

- Algorithms changed. Followings evaporated.
- Platforms started selling writing as AI training data.
- A lot of us looked up and noticed we'd built on land we don't own.
- One sentence on the AI angle is plenty. Don't litigate it.

## The quiet internet

[Name the lineage. Make it sound like a real strand of the web.]

- Personal sites. Hand-coded blogs. Newsletters sent to people who actually wanted them.
- Craig Mod, Robin Sloan, Maggie Appleton — pick the names that matter to you.
- What it has that platforms don't: writing that exists *for the writing.* No algorithm to optimize for.

## What you actually get

[Specifics. Be concrete. This is the section that earns the argument.]

- Pages that don't change shape every six months.
- Readers who decided to be there. Smaller, higher quality.
- A permanent record. Compounding body of work.
- The frame is yours. The medium isn't pre-set by someone else's design system.

## What it costs

[Be honest. The personal site is not free. Naming the cost makes the argument more credible.]

- Time. The first essay is read by approximately nobody. So is the second.
- No metrics-as-feedback loop. No live counter, no like button.
- Operational burden — domain, build, markdown. Small, but not zero.
- Frame all three as features, not bugs — the absence of these is what the platforms had.

## The new luxury

[The thesis. The piece you want to land. Take your time.]

- A platform post in 2026 might be human, AI, or something between. The medium has flattened.
- A personal site is a signal that *someone took the time to put this here.*
- The old luxury was attention. The new luxury is having attention paid in a place you actually own.

## Close

[Land on this site, specifically, if you want to. It's earned.]

- Brief description of what this site is: hairlines, serif type, olive accent. No tracking. No comments. No likes.
- "If you're reading this, you came here on purpose. Thanks. That's the whole pitch." (Or your version.)

---

**Watch for:**

- Don't get nostalgic. "The old web was better" is a weaker frame than "writing on your own land matters now in a way it didn't before."
- Resist itemizing every platform's sins. The argument is structural, not platform-specific.
- The meta-move (this site, right now) only works if it's understated.
