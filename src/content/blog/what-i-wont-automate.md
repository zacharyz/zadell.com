> Outline. This essay is built around a list — the list IS the essay. Each item should be specific, defensible, and personal.
>
> **Voice notes:** Philosophical but grounded. Avoid the Luddite frame; the argument is more interesting because you *do* use AI tools and have decided where the line is.

**Suggested openings:**

- "The question I keep asking myself, this year, is the inverse of the one everyone else seems to be asking."
- "Everyone else is asking *what can I automate.* I keep asking *what won't I.*"
- A specific moment — reaching for the tool faster than the work, and noticing.

---

## The frame

[Brief. Set up the inversion. Establish that you're not anti-AI.]

- Everyone is asking what to automate. The more interesting question is what not to.
- You use AI tools daily. This is not a Luddite essay.
- Somewhere in the last [X] months, you noticed you were reaching for the tool faster than the work. That's where the list started.

---

## The list

[Each item: 1-2 short paragraphs. Name the thing, name why it stays manual, name the version of you it protects.]

### Reading

[Why book summaries are the wrong unit. The journey vs. the destination.]

- The summary gives you the conclusions. The book gives you the path the writer walked.
- Summaries you've read have always been wrong — not factually, but in the sense of having missed what the book was doing.

### First drafts of your own writing

[The drafting *is* the thinking. Skip the work, miss the build.]

- Let the model write the first draft, edit it, and you have a finished artifact and an unchanged mind.
- AI for the parts of writing that aren't writing — research, line editing, finding the word — fine. The composition itself is yours.

### Meaningful conversations

[The polished version isn't what the moment requires.]

- Friend going through something. Colleague in disagreement. Family member you haven't called.
- The temptation: draft it in the assistant first.
- The cost: the version of you who shows up with the wrong words and the long pauses is the version they needed.

### Long runs

[Specific to you — the marathon thread. The model can see the metrics; it cannot feel the legs.]

- The watch has data. The data is useful.
- The interpretation is yours, built from years of running.
- This is partly what threshold.fit is being built against (briefly — don't pitch).

### Decisions that will define the next ten years

[Outsourcing the framing decisions = a small abdication of personhood.]

- What to build. Who to build it with. Where to live.
- These aren't optimization problems. There's no objective function.
- The point of making them is *that you made them.*

---

## Why the list matters

[The thesis. Land it.]

- Not arguing AI is bad or automation is corrosive.
- The opposite: *as more of life becomes automatable, the choice of what stays unautomated becomes the most important choice you make.*
- The list is small. It's supposed to be small.
- The new luxury is not what you have done for you. The new luxury is what you do yourself.

## Close

[Brief. Deliberate choice as a practice. The list is how you practice it.]

---

**Watch for:**

- Specificity matters. "I won't automate hard things" is too vague. Each item needs a defensible claim.
- Resist adding more items. Five is plenty. Six starts to dilute.
- Don't moralize. The strongest version of this essay describes your own practice and lets the reader draw their own line.
